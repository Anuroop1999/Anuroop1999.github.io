Research

Building planetary systems, one scale at a time

A planetary system is assembled across an enormous range of scales, from hundreds of au in the outer disk down to a fraction of an au at the inner rim. I follow that whole range with observations, tracing how a disk of gas and dust reorganises itself into rings, gaps, companions, and eventually planets. The work moves from large ALMA surveys of disk demographics, through the eruptive young stars and embedded companions caught in the act of forming, down to the inner au resolved with optical and infrared interferometry. Tying it together is a set of reduction and modeling pipelines I build so the analysis stays uniform and reproducible across very different instruments.

01 · Outer disks and disk demographics

The outer disk holds most of the solid mass that planets are eventually built from, so its size and structure set the stage for everything that follows. I use high-frequency ALMA continuum imaging to measure how big disks are, how that size depends on evolutionary stage, multiplicity, and environment, and what those trends reveal about how dust drifts, grows, and gets trapped. As part of the ODISEA programme, I produced a uniform set of size measurements for the brightest disks in the Ophiuchus molecular cloud, which let me compare disks across a single, well-defined sample rather than stitching together heterogeneous archival data.

The aim is to turn a snapshot of disk sizes into an evolutionary story: which disks are compact because they are young, which are compact because dust has already drifted inward, and how a stellar companion reshapes the disk it orbits. These demographics feed directly into models of dust evolution and planet formation, and they set the target lists for the higher-resolution follow-up I do at other wavelengths.

  • Uniform disk-size measurements across complete, brightness-selected samples.
  • Size as a function of evolutionary class, multiplicity, and environment.
  • Links between observed morphology and dust drift, grain growth, and dust traps.
  • Demographics that connect to the ODISEA evolutionary sequence of disk substructures.

02 · Embedded companions and early planet formation

Some companions are still forming inside their disks, often buried in dust and still accreting, which makes them both hard to detect and unusually informative. I use L-band high-contrast imaging with VLT/ERIS to search for these deeply embedded companions, with a focus on systems where gravitational instability could fragment the disk into new objects. The clearest example so far is V960 Mon, where my ERIS pipeline recovered a new companion candidate; that result was the basis of an ESO press release in 2025.

A single image is only the start. I combine the imaging with multi-wavelength context, ALMA clumps, scattered-light spirals, and accretion signatures, to judge whether a candidate is a real, bound, forming object rather than a background source or an artefact. Second-epoch astrometry then tests whether a candidate shares the host star's motion, and contrast curves set honest limits on what could still be hiding below the detection threshold.

  • L-band angular differential imaging tuned for deeply embedded, accreting companions.
  • Second-epoch astrometry to test common proper motion and rule out background stars.
  • Photometric calibration and contrast curves that convert non-detections into mass limits.
  • Linking disk clumps and spirals to gravitational-instability fragmentation scenarios.

03 · Eruptive young stars and FU Orionis outbursts

FU Orionis stars are young objects that brighten by orders of magnitude when their disks dump material onto the star in a burst of accretion. These outbursts are short-lived and rare, which makes them a direct, time-resolved window onto how disks feed their stars and how the surrounding dust responds to a sudden flood of heat and light. I work on both halves of the problem: finding new outbursts, and dissecting the disks of known ones.

To find them, I built a multi-stage photometric pipeline that mines the full ZTF archive through the ALeRCE broker, combining infrared colour selection of disk-bearing young stars with amplitude, light-curve shape, and periodicity tests. It recovers the known ZTF-era outbursts and yields the largest uniformly selected sample of candidates to date, with a real-time monitor that flags new events for rapid follow-up and is designed to carry over to the Rubin/LSST alert stream. To understand what an outburst does to a disk, I am combining JWST and ALMA observations of the FUor HBC 687, tracing the heated dust and gas from the inner disk outward.

  • End-to-end ZTF/ALeRCE search recovering known FUors and the largest uniform candidate sample so far.
  • Real-time alerting for target-of-opportunity follow-up, portable to Rubin/LSST.
  • Spectroscopic classification of new eruptive candidates with NTT/EFOSC2.
  • A combined JWST and ALMA view of the FUor HBC 687, from inner-disk dust to the larger envelope.
  • Accepted VISIR observing to study how outbursts process dust into crystalline silicates.

04 · Inner disks on sub-au scales

The innermost disk, the few tenths of an au where dust sublimates and where close companions clear cavities, is far too small to image directly even with the largest single telescopes. Optical and infrared interferometry gets there by combining light from separate telescopes to reach milliarcsecond resolution. I analyse VLTI/GRAVITY and CHARA data to constrain the geometry, asymmetry, and temperature structure of inner disks, and to test how close companions sculpt them.

Much of this is modeling rather than imaging. I fit the interferometric observables, squared visibilities and closure phases, together with the broadband spectral energy distribution, using temperature-gradient disk models alongside simpler multi-component geometric models. That combination pins down inner-rim radii and temperatures, picks out asymmetries and binarity through closure-phase signals, and lets me study inner-disk structure statistically across a sample of Herbig Ae/Be stars rather than one object at a time.

  • Temperature-gradient disk models fit jointly to near-infrared visibilities and the SED.
  • Multi-component geometric models for star, inner rim, halo, and compact emission.
  • Closure-phase diagnostics of asymmetry and binarity in the inner disk.
  • A statistical, sample-level study of Herbig Ae/Be inner-disk structure.

Methods and pipelines

High-contrast imaging

Angular and reference differential imaging, PSF subtraction, injection-and-recovery tests, contrast-to-mass conversion, and precise astrometry for companion searches.

Optical/IR interferometry

Visibility and closure-phase modeling, temperature-gradient and multi-component fits, photometric consistency checks, and careful propagation of uncertainties.

Millimeter imaging

Uniform measurement of disk sizes and fluxes, control of beam and sensitivity effects, and sample-level statistics across complete disk samples.

Time-domain surveys

Large-scale archive queries through the ALeRCE broker, light-curve selection and periodicity tests, and real-time alerting built to scale to Rubin/LSST.

Spectral-cube analysis

JWST/MIRI IFU reduction, continuum fitting, extinction correction, and dust, ice, and outflow diagnostics for eruptive young stars.

Statistical inference

Bayesian parameter estimation with MCMC, bootstrap and Monte Carlo error analysis, and reproducible, instrument-adaptable pipelines with structured, versioned outputs.
